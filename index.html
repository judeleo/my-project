The effect of martial arts training on the performance of football skills among football players in University of Ibadan

Oparachukwu, J. A., & Fadoju, A. O. (2024)

The Journal of Positive Psychology and Counselling, Vol. 14, pp. 117 129

Applied training intervention examining martial arts transfer effects on football skill performance.

Read →

This study seeks to understand the effect of Taekwondo martial art training on physical skill among University of Ibadan Football Players. The study assessed the effect of 6 weeks Taekwondo (Martial Arts) Skill training on football players. Non-probability purposive sampling was adopted. Instruments included the Sport Aggression Questionnaire and the SMTQ-18. Results revealed significant influence of Taekwondo training on physical skill/Instrumental Aggression of University of Ibadan football players as indicated by the F-statistics value of 65.49 (p=0.000 <0.05). Results suggest that martial arts training offers a viable cross-training tool for enhancing football technical skills with implications for sport-specific conditioning and holistic athlete development.

2024

Effect of Taekwondo Mental Toughness Skill Training on Assertive Behaviour among University of Ibadan Football Players

Oparachukwu, J. A. (2024)

IN: Department of Human Kinetics and Health Education, University of Ibadan

Psychological skills intervention linking Taekwondo mental toughness training to assertive performance behaviour in football.

Read →

Football is a sport that has grown and developed over the years. This study sought to understand the effect of Taekwondo mental toughness skill training on assertive behaviour among University of Ibadan Football Players. Twenty-four (24) players from the Faculty of Education were selected using non-probability purposive sampling. Instruments included the Sport Aggression Questionnaire and the 18-item Sport Mental Toughness Questionnaire (SMTQ-18). Regression analysis shows a significant result: Taekwondo Martial Art skills training has a significant effect on assertive behaviour in game situations. ANOVA shows F(3, 246) = 26.86, P<0.01. Findings indicate that Taekwondo mental toughness skill training significantly influences the Instrumental Aggression of University of Ibadan football players.

Dr. Oparachukwu earned a Bachelor's degree in Human Kinetics, a Master's degree in the Sociology and Psychology of Sport, and a PhD in Performance Psychology from the University of Ibadan, Nigeria. His scholarly and applied work has focused on themes such as cognitive reframing, verbal affirmation, mental toughness, athlete mistake response, and high-performance system design. He has contributed chapters to scholarly books and published research in areas spanning psychological skills interventions, athlete development, and sport performance.

Beyond consulting and research, Dr. Oparachukwu has built a growing digital presence as an educator and thought leader. Through his YouTube platform and educational content, he teaches practical lessons on confidence, focus, self-talk, pressure management, performance slumps, leadership, and human potential. He is also the creator of #AskDrJoshuaOparachukwu, a question-and-response platform through which athletes and performers from around the world can seek guidance on performance-related challenges.

At the heart of his work is a simple conviction: excellence is trainable. Whether working with an athlete preparing for competition, a coach building a performance culture, or an individual seeking greater consistency in life and work, Dr. Oparachukwu is devoted to helping people perform closer to their potential, more often, and with greater purpose.

Case Study 01 of 16
Athlete Performance · Case Study 01

Learning to Work With Fear,
Not Against It

Youth Academy Forward · Elite FC · Early 20s · Competitive Football Environment

The Situation

The athlete came into the work skeptical. That's the first thing worth saying, because it matters. He wasn't someone who had read about performance psychology or sought it out. He was a footballer with a specific problem. He tightened up in high-stakes games and he wasn't entirely convinced that talking about it would change anything.

That skepticism was actually useful. It meant that whatever shifted during the work, shifted because it was real, not because he wanted to believe it would work.

The problem he described was familiar in its shape but specific in its texture. In big games matches with large crowds, high stakes, or difficult opposition something would happen to his body that he couldn't fully explain. He'd tighten. His decision-making would slow. The natural rhythm he played with in training would disappear, replaced by something more hesitant and reactive. He knew he was capable of better. The gap between what he could do and what he was doing in those moments was the thing that brought him into the work.

What Made This Case Complex

The temptation with a case like this is to treat it as an anxiety problem and work backward from there. But that framing would have been wrong and, more importantly, unhelpful.

He wasn't anxious in the clinical sense. He was a competitive athlete in high-pressure environments responding in a way that is, physiologically, completely normal. The elevation in arousal he experienced before big games the tightness, the hyperawareness was his nervous system doing exactly what it is designed to do. The problem wasn't the feeling. The problem was his relationship with the feeling.

He had spent years trying to suppress it, ignore it, or talk himself out of it. None of it worked, because none of it addressed what was actually happening. You cannot think your way out of a physiological state. You can only learn to move through it.

The working hypothesis that emerged was this: the issue was less technical than cognitive. Mistakes were being interpreted as threats rather than neutral performance events, triggering emotional carryover that bled into every subsequent action. One bad touch wasn't just a bad touch. It became evidence. And evidence accumulates.

That reframe from something is wrong with me to my body is preparing me and I don't yet know how to use that preparation was the first and most important shift of the entire intervention.

The Intervention

Once the perceptual work was done, a structured behavioral framework was introduced: the 3R Performance Reset.

The model has three components, each designed to operate in real time on the pitch fast enough to be useful, simple enough to be reliable under pressure.

Recognize

See mistakes clearly, without the layer of self-judgment. A missed chance is a missed chance. It is not a verdict.

Reframe

Shift interpretation from threat to information. Every error contains data about what the next action should be.

Re-engage

Shift attention deliberately to the next task. Not when it feels right. Not after the emotion has passed. Now.

Supporting this framework, verbal affirmation routines were introduced specific internal language the athlete could use in the seconds following a mistake to interrupt the old pattern and activate the reset. The language was precise, personal, and practiced until it became automatic.

The full performance flow the intervention targeted looked like this: Mistake → Interpretation → Response → Performance Outcome. The only variable the athlete could control was the middle two. That's where all the work lived.

What Changed

The shift wasn't dramatic. That's worth saying plainly. But what changed was more durable than drama. The athlete began to notice the familiar tightening arriving in big games but instead of fighting it or fearing it, he recognized it for what it was. He had language for it. He had something to do with it. The window between feeling the pressure and being consumed by it widened. Inside that window, he could perform.

Across the intervention period, clear and observable shifts emerged: faster recovery after errors, improved in-game decision-making, reduced emotional carryover between mistakes, and greater composure in the moments that had previously undone him.

His Coach specifically noted improved engagement and stability in the second halves of difficult matches the period where emotional fatigue had historically cost him most.

Key Performance Insight

"Mistakes do not damage performance nearly as much as the athlete's interpretation of mistakes. The recovery window is not a talent problem. It is a habit problem. And habits can be built."

Mistakes were being interpreted as threats rather than neutral performance events, triggering emotional carryover that bled into every subsequent action. One bad touch wasn't just a bad touch. It became evidence. And evidence accumulates.

That reframe from "something is wrong with me" to "my body is preparing me and I don't yet know how to use that preparation" was the first and most important shift of the entire intervention.

The Intervention: 3R Performance Reset

Once the perceptual work was done, a structured behavioral framework was introduced: the 3R Performance Reset.

The model has three components, each designed to operate in real time on the pitch fast enough to be useful, simple enough to be reliable under pressure.

R

Recognize

The athlete was first taught to see mistakes clearly, without the layer of self-judgment that had always accompanied them. A missed chance is a missed chance. It is not a verdict. This sounds straightforward. For most athletes, it requires deliberate, repeated practice before it becomes instinctive.

R

Reframe

The second step involved changing what mistakes meant. Not dismissing them, not pretending they didn't happen, but shifting their interpretation from threat to information. Every error contains data about what the next action should be. Accessing that data requires a mind that isn't in crisis.

R

Re-engage

The third step was the most concrete: re-engage immediately. Shift attention deliberately and rapidly to the next task, the next decision, the next action. Not when it feels right. Not after the emotion has passed. Now. The re-engagement was anchored to physical cues already present in the game.

Supporting this framework, verbal affirmation routines were introduced specific internal language the athlete could use in the seconds following a mistake to interrupt the old pattern and activate the reset. The language was precise, personal, and practiced until it became automatic.

The full performance flow the intervention targeted looked like this:

Mistake → Interpretation → Response → Performance Outcome

The only variable the athlete could control was the middle two. That's where all the work lived.

What Changed

The shift wasn't dramatic. That's worth saying plainly. But what changed was more durable than drama. The athlete began to notice the familiar tightening arriving in big games but instead of fighting it or fearing it, he recognized it for what it was. He had language for it. He had something to do with it. The window between feeling the pressure and being consumed by it widened. Inside that window, he could perform.

Across the intervention period, clear and observable shifts emerged: faster recovery after errors, improved in-game decision-making, reduced emotional carryover between mistakes, and greater composure in the moments that had previously undone him.

His Coach specifically noted improved engagement and stability in the second halves of difficult matches the period where emotional fatigue had historically cost him most.

Key Performance Insight

Mistakes do not damage performance nearly as much as the athlete's interpretation of mistakes. The recovery window is not a talent problem. It is a habit problem. And habits can be built.

Case Study 02 · Athlete Performance

Rebuilding Confidence After a Performance Slump

Competitive Footballer · Attacking Player · Senior Competitive Environment

The Situation

The athlete presented following an extended dip in performance that had gradually become more psychological than technical. What began as a run of poor outcomes had developed into something more disruptive. Missed chances were carrying over into subsequent actions, confidence had become fragile, and decision-making in attacking moments was increasingly hesitant.

The athlete described feeling as though he had lost a version of himself that had once felt natural. In training, flashes of quality remained. In matches especially when early moments did not go well doubt appeared quickly and began to shape choices in real time. He was not performing freely. He was performing cautiously.

The issue was not a complete loss of ability. It was an unstable relationship with performance itself.

What Made This Case Complex

Performance slumps are often misunderstood because they can appear purely technical from the outside. A player looks out of form and the assumption becomes that more repetition or more correction is needed.

But this case suggested something different.

The athlete was no longer simply responding to what was happening in the game. He was responding to what he feared might happen next. That anticipatory layer was affecting perception, narrowing options, and producing decisions driven more by avoidance than opportunity.

The working hypothesis was that confidence had become contingent. Good moments created temporary belief. Mistakes or missed chances dismantled it. Performance was being governed by fluctuations in self-evaluation rather than stable internal processes.

The problem was not just confidence loss. It was confidence dependency.

The Intervention: 3A Performance Anchor

Intervention centred on stabilizing confidence by shifting it away from outcomes and back toward controllable performance processes. A structured framework was introduced: the 3A Performance Anchor.

A

Anchor One Return to Controllables

Attention was redirected away from outcomes, approval, or immediate success, and toward controllable actions within the game: movement, scanning, timing, positioning, and decision cues. The athlete could not control whether a chance went in. He could control how he moved, when he committed, and where his attention sat.

A

Anchor Two Rebuild Functional Confidence

Confidence was treated not as a feeling to wait for, but as something built through repeated engagement with controllable actions. The athlete practised acting with commitment before feeling fully certain. Functional confidence does not require certainty. It requires consistency of process.

A

Anchor Three Compete Forward

Following errors or poor moments, attention was trained to move forward rapidly into the next action rather than backward into evaluation. The past cannot be changed inside a match. The next action always can.

Supporting this framework, verbal affirmation routines were paired with performance cue words to interrupt self-doubt and reinforce task focus during live competition. These were not generic affirmations they were precise, personally constructed phrases tied to the specific moments where the athlete's attention had historically collapsed.

The full performance flow the intervention targeted looked like this:

Event→ Interpretation→ Attention→ Decision→ Performance Outcome

The work centred on changing the middle sequence. The event and the outcome were not the variables. Everything between them was.

What Changed

Over the intervention period, confidence became less volatile. The athlete showed reduced hesitation in attacking decisions, improved commitment in key moments, and greater emotional stability following missed chances.

There was a noticeable reduction in overthinking during performance. Choices became quicker. Play became freer. Rather than protecting against mistakes, the athlete began competing again.

Coach observations also suggested a return of assertiveness in attacking actions and more stable performance across matches rather than isolated flashes. The fluctuations did not disappear entirely. But their effect on subsequent performance diminished significantly.

Key Performance Insight

Confidence becomes durable the moment it stops depending on outcomes and starts depending on process. That shift is not motivational. It is structural. Build it that way.

Case Study 03 · Athlete Performance

When Preparation Became Paralysis

Competitive Footballer · Goalkeeper · Senior Competitive Environment

The Situation

The athlete presented with a problem that appeared, on the surface, to be discipline. He prepared intensely. He reviewed opponents. Rehearsed scenarios. Mentally ran through contingencies before matches. Coaches valued his seriousness.

But preparation had begun hardening into something else. On match days, he was arriving overloaded.

Instead of preparation creating readiness, it was creating tension. He was carrying too many possibilities into performance. Too many "what if" scenarios. Too many imagined failures before a ball had been kicked. His body was prepared. His attention was crowded.

The athlete described entering games feeling as though he had already played the match in his head and lost parts of it. That was the problem. Preparation was no longer organizing performance. It was interfering with it.

What Made This Case Complex

This case was complex because the behavior looked adaptive. Preparation is usually praised. Detail orientation is usually praised. Anticipation is usually praised.

The problem was not the existence of those traits. It was excess. Preparation had crossed a threshold where it no longer sharpened attention. It fragmented it.

The athlete was entering competition trying to hold too many variables consciously opponent tendencies, set-piece threats, distribution choices, communication responsibilities, recovery scenarios.

This was not performance anxiety in the conventional sense. It was anticipatory overload.

The working hypothesis was that cognitive load before competition was exhausting attentional resources needed during competition itself. The issue was not insufficient preparation. It was preparation without release.

The Intervention: 3S Competitive Readiness Model

Intervention centred on reducing pre-competition overload and restoring selective attention through a structured framework called the 3S Competitive Readiness Model.

S

Simplify

Pre-match preparation was reduced rather than expanded. Information was narrowed to a small number of essential performance priorities. Not everything deserved equal attention.

S

Select

The athlete was trained to identify what belonged in conscious awareness and what had to remain trusted background skill. Selection replaced accumulation.

S

Settle

A pre-performance settling sequence was introduced to transition from preparation mode into performance mode. The aim was not relaxation. It was attentional readiness.

Supporting this framework, match-day cue routines were built around brief attentional resets linked to moments already present in the goalkeeper's role: before goal kicks, before defensive set pieces, and in the pause before restarts.

The performance flow the intervention targeted looked like this:

Preparation Load→ Attentional Crowding→ Decision Noise→ Hesitation→ Performance Outcome

The work centred on interrupting that sequence before the match even began.

What Changed

Over the intervention period, the athlete entered matches appearing less mentally strained and more present. Communication became clearer. Distribution decisions became less delayed. Responses in transition moments became quicker.

Most importantly, the athlete reported feeling available to the game again rather than occupied by thoughts about the game.

Coach observations suggested improved composure in early match phases historically the period where overload had shown most strongly.

The athlete did not become less prepared. He became less burdened by preparation. That changed performance.

Key Performance Insight

Preparation improves performance only until it exceeds what attention can hold. Beyond that point, more preparation can reduce readiness. High performance often depends not only on what an athlete adds before competition, but what the athlete knows to leave out.

This study seeks to understand the effect of Taekwondo martial art training on physical skill among University of Ibadan Football Players. The study assessed the effect of 6 weeks Taekwondo (Martial Arts) Skill training on University of Ibadan football players' physical skill. Non-probability purposive sampling was adopted. Instruments included the Sport Aggression Questionnaire and the 18-item Sport Mental Toughness Questionnaire (SMTQ-18). Data was analyzed using descriptive and inferential statistics. Case Study 14 · Executive / High-Pressure Performance

When Decision Speed Became Decision Fatigue

Founder · Growth-Stage Business Environment · Executive Performance and Decision Load

4Q Executive Decision FilterDecision ArchitectureCognitive Load

The Situation

The client was not struggling because he lacked ambition or capacity. He was driving growth, managing people, handling client expectations, making financial decisions, and still trying to remain creatively and strategically available to the business. From the outside, he looked functional. Inside the work, a different pattern became clear.

The moment that made it undeniable came during a conversation about a pricing decision the client had been sitting with for eleven days. It was not a complex decision by any objective measure — but it had not moved. When asked what was blocking it, the client could not immediately say. There was no missing information. There was just the decision, sitting in the queue alongside thirty others.

His day had become a sequence of decisions with very little recovery between them. By the time he reached the decisions that mattered most, he was often already depleted. The client described it precisely: the business had not exceeded his intelligence. It had exceeded his available bandwidth.

What Made This Case Complex

The client's responsiveness had been one of the primary reasons the business had grown. He moved quickly. He solved problems personally. He was accessible to staff and clients in ways that built genuine trust. But the same responsiveness that had built the business had become a performance liability.

The business had learned to send too many decisions upward, because upward had always meant faster. Staff waited for approval on issues that could have been resolved closer to execution. The client was not simply making too many decisions. He had become the organisation's decision filter.

The working diagnosis was that executive fatigue was being produced less by workload volume alone and more by decision architecture. Too many decisions were reaching the wrong level. The issue was not time management. It was decision load design.

The Intervention: 4Q Executive Decision Filter

Qualify

Classify decisions by consequence level — low-risk operational, reversible management, high-impact strategic, and identity-defining. A significant number of daily decisions belonged in the first two categories.

Queue

Create deliberate timing for decision review rather than allowing decisions to interrupt attention as they arrived. Protecting thinking quality meant some decisions had to wait.

Delegate

Establish which decisions should no longer reach the founder at all. Not withdrawal — a structural correction returning decisions to the level where accountability could live appropriately.

Decide

Focus on decision closure — identifying when enough information is available and distinguishing that from the pull toward further delay. A decision held open beyond sufficient information is costing the business while pretending to protect it.

Decision Volume → Cognitive Switching → Executive Fatigue → Delayed Decisions → Business Friction

What Changed

Decisions that had previously sat unresolved for days began to close faster — not because the framework made them easier, but because the client was arriving at those decisions with available attention rather than exhausted attention.

As delegation became explicit and documented, staff who had previously brought decisions to the founder as a default began resolving more issues independently. Ownership at the right level became the norm rather than the exception.

The client reported arriving at important conversations — investor discussions, senior hires, major client negotiations — with more of himself available. Not more time. More capacity. The difference between those two things is the difference between being in the room and being present in it.

Key Performance Insight

High-pressure leadership is not only about making better decisions. It is about designing a system in which the right decisions reach the right level of attention. Decision fatigue is frequently misread as a personal limitation. In reality it is usually a structural problem, produced by an architecture that routes too many decisions to too few people.

Case Study 15 · Executive / High-Pressure Performance

When Public Confidence Hid Private Overextension

Senior Executive · High-Visibility Leadership Environment · Pressure, Image and Performance Sustainability

3S Executive SustainabilityLeadership SustainabilityConcealment and Composure

The Situation

The executive presented as composed, articulate, and highly capable. In meetings, he was clear. In public-facing moments, he was confident. His team experienced him as dependable, strategic, and emotionally steady. That was part of the difficulty. Because the version of him that others saw was still functioning, the cost of maintaining that version had become invisible.

The moment that brought it into focus came at the end of a particularly demanding week. When asked how he actually felt at the end of it, the answer came slowly and with visible effort: he did not know. Not because the question was difficult, but because the practice of noticing — of attending to his own internal state rather than managing its presentation — had become genuinely unfamiliar.

Privately, he was stretched thin. Carrying pressure from multiple directions: organisational targets, staff expectations, board-level scrutiny, public visibility, and the need to appear certain even when decisions were genuinely complex. The question was how long he could keep delivering at that cost, and what would be left when the cost finally exceeded the reserve.

What Made This Case Complex

This case was difficult because the executive's competence was masking the problem. Underperformance would have made intervention easier. But the executive was still producing results, still leading meetings, still making decisions. The issue was not that he was failing. The issue was that effectiveness had become dependent on concealment.

He had learned to manage pressure by tightening control over how much of him was visible. Uncertainty was edited. Fatigue was concealed. Emotional strain was converted into productivity. The organisation saw strength, but much of that strength was being financed by private depletion.

The specific trap of high-functioning concealment is that it does not feel like suppression from the inside. It feels like professionalism. It feels like responsibility. It feels like exactly the kind of leadership the role requires. The risk is not immediate failure. It is progressive internal cost that is never examined because the external performance never triggers an alarm.

The Intervention: 3S Executive Sustainability Model

Separate

Distinguish between what leadership required him to show publicly and what he needed to acknowledge privately. Composure was not dismantled — it was treated as a legitimate role demand. What had to change was the absence of any private counterpart to it.

Signal

Introduce calibrated transparency — developing language that could convey the genuine complexity of difficult decisions without creating the impression of uncertainty. Naming complexity clearly is not the same as expressing doubt. It is a more sophisticated form of confidence.

Sustain

Recovery architecture — the practical design of conditions in which the executive's internal state could be maintained rather than progressively depleted. Recovery framed not as a reward for completed work, but as a professional discipline that made the work possible.

External Pressure → Public Composure → Private Suppression → Internal Cost → Sustainability Risk

What Changed

He began naming the genuine difficulty of complex decisions before stating his direction on them. The response from colleagues surprised him. Rather than reading the acknowledgment of complexity as weakness, senior peers described the change as an increase in credibility.

As recovery architecture became a practiced discipline rather than a concession, the spillover that had previously followed demanding periods shortened noticeably. He was not recovering faster because he had become more resilient. He was recovering faster because he had stopped treating recovery as optional.

The executive reported that leadership became less effortful — not in the sense that the demands reduced, but in the sense that maintaining his presence under those demands stopped requiring the same level of internal management. The composure that had previously been performed was becoming something closer to actual.

Key Performance Insight

Appearing unaffected is not the same as being well-regulated, and the distance between those two things is where progressive cost accumulates. Sustainable leadership is not built by hiding the cost of high performance. It is built by refusing to let that cost remain unexamined.

Case Study 16 · Executive / High-Pressure Performance

When Urgency Became the Operating Culture

Managing Director · Fast-Growth Organization · Executive Focus and Organizational Tempo

3P Executive TempoUrgency CultureOrganizational Rhythm

The Situation

The executive came into the work describing a business that was moving quickly, but not always moving well. The organisation had ambition. It had capable people. It had opportunities in front of it. On the surface, the speed looked like momentum.

But inside the organisation, urgency had become the default setting. The moment that made the pattern impossible to dismiss came during what should have been a routine strategic planning session. Within forty minutes, the session had been interrupted three times. The strategic conversation never recovered. The session ended with the decision deferred.

Everything felt important. Messages were answered immediately. Meetings were scheduled quickly, often without enough preparation. The executive had begun to notice that speed was no longer only a response to genuine pressure. It had become part of the culture. Acceleration had stopped being chosen. It had become automatic.

What Made This Case Complex

Urgency had produced results in the past — and the executive knew it. In the early stages of growth, fast response had helped the organisation win trust. Speed became something the organisation was proud of. But a strength had started to overextend itself.

The team had learned to treat speed as seriousness. The faster someone replied, the more committed they appeared. Slowness had begun to feel like weakness, even when the situation required thought rather than reaction. The organisation was not only working fast. It was beginning to think poorly because it had lost permission to slow down.

Decisions that needed reflection were being handled with response energy. Strategic questions were being treated like operational emergencies. Staff were becoming good at reacting and less good at prioritising. The issue was not urgency itself. It was urgency without discrimination.

The Intervention: 3P Executive Tempo Model

Pause

Create structured space between stimulus and response — a moment of classification before action. Separating issues that required immediate action from those that simply felt uncomfortable to leave unanswered.

Prioritize

Rank decisions and tasks by actual consequence rather than felt urgency. Ask whether an issue affected revenue, reputation, people, delivery, compliance, or long-term strategy. If not, it could not behave like a crisis.

Pace

Design different operating speeds for different types of work and make those speeds explicit. Operational issues had one rhythm. Strategic decisions had another. Internal reflection had a third.

Incoming Demand → Urgency Interpretation → Response Speed → Decision Quality → Organisational Rhythm

What Changed

Strategic sessions began to hold their time. The decision that had been deferred in the opening session was revisited six weeks into the intervention and completed in a single 90-minute meeting that was not interrupted once.

Staff brought fewer issues upward, but the issues they brought were better defined, had already been partially worked through, and arrived with a clearer sense of what was being asked. The executive was spending less time receiving problems and more time making decisions.

The fragmentation that had characterised his working weeks — the sense of being perpetually interrupted, of never completing a thought before the next demand arrived — reduced. The organisation had not slowed down. It had become more rhythmically intelligent.

Key Performance Insight

Speed is valuable only when it serves judgment. When every issue is treated as urgent, urgency stops guiding action and starts weakening attention. High-pressure organisations do not need to move slowly. They need to know which moments require speed, which require sustained thought, and which require the discipline to recognise that discomfort is not the same as importance.
