Effect of taekwondo mental toughness skill training on assertive behaviour among University of Ibadan football players

Oparachukwu, J. A., & Fadoju, A. O. (2024)

IN: Legacy of Excellence in Education and Sports Management — Festschrift for Prof. E. O. Morakinyo, pp. 107–121

Psychological skills intervention linking mental toughness training to assertive performance behavior in football.

Read →

Football is a sport that has grown and developed over the years, experiencing dynamism in its style of play, rules, and tactics. This study sought to understand the effect of Taekwondo mental toughness skill training on assertive behaviour among University of Ibadan Football Players. Twenty-four (24) players from the Faculty of Education were selected using non-probability purposive sampling. Instruments included the Sport Aggression Questionnaire and the 18-item Sport Mental Toughness Questionnaire (SMTQ-18). Analysis using Regression showed a significant result: Taekwondo Martial Art skills training has a significant effect on assertive behaviour in game situations. ANOVA shows F(3, 246) = 26.86, P<0.01. Findings indicate that Taekwondo mental toughness skill training significantly influences the Instrumental Aggression of University of Ibadan football players.

The effect of martial arts training on the performance of football skills among football players in University of Ibadan

Oparachukwu, J. A., & Fadoju, A. O. (2024)

The Journal of Positive Psychology and Counselling, Vol. 14, pp. 117–129

Applied training intervention examining martial arts transfer effects on football skill performance.

Read →

This study seeks to understand the effect of Taekwondo martial art training on physical skill among University of Ibadan Football Players. The study assessed the effect of 6 weeks Taekwondo (Martial Arts) Skill training on football players. Non-probability purposive sampling was adopted. Instruments included the Sport Aggression Questionnaire and the SMTQ-18. Results revealed significant influence of Taekwondo training on physical skill/Instrumental Aggression of University of Ibadan football players as indicated by the F-statistics value of 65.49 (p=0.000 <0.05). Results suggest that martial arts training offers a viable cross-training tool for enhancing football technical skills with implications for sport-specific conditioning and holistic athlete development.

Athlete Performance · Case Study 01

Learning to Work With Fear,
Not Against It

Youth Academy Forward · Elite FC · Early 20s · Competitive Football Environment

The Situation

The athlete came into the work skeptical. That's the first thing worth saying, because it matters. He wasn't someone who had read about performance psychology or sought it out. He was a footballer with a specific problem. He tightened up in high-stakes games and he wasn't entirely convinced that talking about it would change anything.

That skepticism was actually useful. It meant that whatever shifted during the work, shifted because it was real, not because he wanted to believe it would work.

The problem he described was familiar in its shape but specific in its texture. In big games — matches with large crowds, high stakes, or difficult opposition — something would happen to his body that he couldn't fully explain. He'd tighten. His decision-making would slow. The natural rhythm he played with in training would disappear, replaced by something more hesitant and reactive. He knew he was capable of better. The gap between what he could do and what he was doing in those moments was the thing that brought him into the work.

What Made This Case Complex

The temptation with a case like this is to treat it as an anxiety problem and work backward from there. But that framing would have been wrong and, more importantly, unhelpful.

He wasn't anxious in the clinical sense. He was a competitive athlete in high-pressure environments responding in a way that is, physiologically, completely normal. The elevation in arousal he experienced before big games — the tightness, the hyperawareness — was his nervous system doing exactly what it is designed to do. The problem wasn't the feeling. The problem was his relationship with the feeling.

He had spent years trying to suppress it, ignore it, or talk himself out of it. None of it worked, because none of it addressed what was actually happening. You cannot think your way out of a physiological state. You can only learn to move through it.

The working hypothesis that emerged was this: the issue was less technical than cognitive. Mistakes were being interpreted as threats rather than neutral performance events, triggering emotional carryover that bled into every subsequent action. One bad touch wasn't just a bad touch. It became evidence. And evidence accumulates.

That reframe — from something is wrong with me to my body is preparing me and I don't yet know how to use that preparation — was the first and most important shift of the entire intervention.

The Intervention

Once the perceptual work was done, a structured behavioral framework was introduced: the 3R Performance Reset.

The model has three components, each designed to operate in real time on the pitch — fast enough to be useful, simple enough to be reliable under pressure.

Recognize

See mistakes clearly, without the layer of self-judgment. A missed chance is a missed chance. It is not a verdict.

Reframe

Shift interpretation from threat to information. Every error contains data about what the next action should be.

Re-engage

Shift attention deliberately to the next task. Not when it feels right. Not after the emotion has passed. Now.

Supporting this framework, verbal affirmation routines were introduced — specific internal language the athlete could use in the seconds following a mistake to interrupt the old pattern and activate the reset. The language was precise, personal, and practiced until it became automatic.

The full performance flow the intervention targeted looked like this: Mistake → Interpretation → Response → Performance Outcome. The only variable the athlete could control was the middle two. That's where all the work lived.

What Changed

The shift wasn't dramatic. That's worth saying plainly. But what changed was more durable than drama. The athlete began to notice the familiar tightening arriving in big games — but instead of fighting it or fearing it, he recognized it for what it was. He had language for it. He had something to do with it. The window between feeling the pressure and being consumed by it widened. Inside that window, he could perform.

Across the intervention period, clear and observable shifts emerged: faster recovery after errors, improved in-game decision-making, reduced emotional carryover between mistakes, and greater composure in the moments that had previously undone him.

His Coach specifically noted improved engagement and stability in the second halves of difficult matches — the period where emotional fatigue had historically cost him most.

Key Performance Insight

"Mistakes do not damage performance nearly as much as the athlete's interpretation of mistakes. The recovery window is not a talent problem. It is a habit problem. And habits can be built."

Case Study 02 · Athlete Performance

Rebuilding Confidence After a Performance Slump

Competitive Footballer · Attacking Player · Senior Competitive Environment

The Situation

The athlete presented following an extended dip in performance that had gradually become more psychological than technical. What began as a run of poor outcomes had developed into something more disruptive. Missed chances were carrying over into subsequent actions, confidence had become fragile, and decision-making in attacking moments was increasingly hesitant.

The athlete described feeling as though he had lost a version of himself that had once felt natural. In training, flashes of quality remained. In matches — especially when early moments did not go well — doubt appeared quickly and began to shape choices in real time. He was not performing freely. He was performing cautiously.

The issue was not a complete loss of ability. It was an unstable relationship with performance itself.

What Made This Case Complex

Performance slumps are often misunderstood because they can appear purely technical from the outside. A player looks out of form and the assumption becomes that more repetition or more correction is needed.

But this case suggested something different.

The athlete was no longer simply responding to what was happening in the game. He was responding to what he feared might happen next. That anticipatory layer was affecting perception, narrowing options, and producing decisions driven more by avoidance than opportunity.

The working hypothesis was that confidence had become contingent. Good moments created temporary belief. Mistakes or missed chances dismantled it. Performance was being governed by fluctuations in self-evaluation rather than stable internal processes.

The senior players delivering feedback were not wrong to demand more. Their standards were appropriate. The problem was that the relational infrastructure to support those standards had not been built alongside them. The expectation had arrived before the environment that makes it receivable.

A structured framework was introduced: the 3A Accountability Culture Model.

Align

Accountability grounded in specific football behaviors — reaction speed after losing possession, communication quality after mistakes, body language when substituted, training intensity in the final 20 minutes. Named and visible. Something to measure rather than aspire to.

Language shift: from judgment of person to observation of action. 'You are not working' became 'the recovery run was late.' The instruction was not simply to change the words — it was to change the intent behind them: from expressing frustration at a person to delivering information about a performance.

Activity created the appearance of progress. That made it harder for the leadership team to see the cost of weak structure — and harder still to name it without appearing ungrateful for the effort that had kept things running.

Process

Decision pathways and repeatable routines for situations the program encountered regularly: how are players admitted, who communicates match changes, how are complaints handled, who approves expenditure, how are coaches reviewed. Figuring things out in advance is more reliable than figuring them out under pressure.

The Situation

The organization did not appear leaderless. There was a strong central figure. Decisions moved quickly when that person acted. Staff deferred to that authority. From the outside, and often from the inside, this looked like an organization that knew how to move.

The subsequent conversation revealed the deeper problem: there was no document, no structure, and no shared understanding that could resolve the question of who had been right. Because the answer, honestly examined, was that the system had never been clear enough for either of them to have been definitively wrong. The organization had authority. It did not yet have decision rights.

What Made This Case Complex

Concentration of authority had originally solved real problems. In the earlier phases of growth, fast centralized decisions had been genuinely valuable. Culture that has produced results is the hardest kind to examine honestly.

Success had disguised structural weakness. As long as the central figure was available and making good decisions, the absence of defined decision rights was invisible. The system worked because one person made it work. That is not a governance model. It is a dependency.

The specific governance risk was not open disorder — the organization was not in chaos. It was hidden contradiction. Two people could each believe they had authority over the same decision. Inside each domain, four types of authority separated: who recommends, who approves, who executes, who monitors. Approval authority reframed not as power but as accountability — the person who approves a decision is the person who carries it if it fails.

Delegate

Where authority could appropriately move away from the center. When authority is delegated with clear documentation, the central figure is not removed from consequence — they are protected from consequence for decisions they were not positioned to make well. Delegation, properly structured, is distribution of appropriate risk.

A decision register and governance matrix. Significant decisions logged: decision made, authority exercised, rationale recorded, accountability assigned. An undocumented decision cannot be learned from, audited, or defended if challenged.

When Policy Existed but Enforcement Had No Spine

Football Organization · Academy and Development Environment · Safeguarding, Discipline and Governance Enforcement

4E Governance EnforcementPolicy CredibilityInstitutional Consistency

The Situation

The organization had policies. There was a code of conduct. Safeguarding statements. Disciplinary expectations for players and staff. On paper, the organization could show that rules existed. But the rules were not carrying authority in practice.

A parent complaint about a coach's conduct was received by two different administrators and handled entirely differently. The first documented it, acknowledged it formally, and escalated it. The second spoke to the coach informally, decided there was no malicious intent, and closed the matter without documentation. Both believed they had handled it correctly — and because there was no enforcement pathway telling either of them what the right process was, both were probably right that no explicit rule had been broken.

Elsewhere, players arrived late without consistent consequence. Welfare concerns were absorbed informally because raising them through the proper channel felt like an escalation nobody wanted. The organization had standards it could cite but not standards it could reliably enforce.

What Made This Case Complex

Almost everyone involved believed their approach was the compassionate one. Coaches wanted to understand background before applying consequences. Administrators did not want families to feel attacked. Those concerns were not wrong. In youth and development environments, rigid enforcement without context causes real harm.

But the opposite problem had taken hold gradually enough that nobody had named it. Context had become a reason not to act. Every case became exceptional. Every breach had a story. The harm of over-enforcement felt immediate and visible. The harm of under-enforcement accumulated invisibly across dozens of small moments where people learned that written expectations did not produce predictable outcomes.

The working diagnosis: the organization had mistaken discretion for governance. Discretion without an enforcement logic that others can observe and trust becomes inconsistency. And inconsistency is where trust leaks — in three directions simultaneously: players receiving mixed signals about what standards meant, parents receiving unequal experiences of fairness, and staff carrying decisions personally because the institution was not strong enough to carry them on anyone's behalf.

The Intervention

A structured framework was introduced: the 4E Governance Enforcement Model.

Establish

Sort existing policies into two categories: those requiring consistent enforcement regardless of context, and those where contextual flexibility was appropriate and intended. Clarify which rules were non-negotiable and what non-negotiable meant in practice. An enforcement process that reserves the right to make exceptions in every case is not an enforcement process — it is a negotiation.

Escalate

A documented escalation pathway. Minor issues begin with informal correction, but repeated or serious issues move through staged documentation rather than successive private conversations. A record is not a verdict. It is institutional memory. Without it, the organization cannot track patterns, demonstrate consistency, or protect itself when a situation turns out not to have been minor.

Evaluate

Before consequences, review four things: context, pattern, risk, and precedent. The critical distinction: context that informs a proportionate response versus context that justifies inaction. Both feel like compassion from the inside. Only one of them is.

Execute

A consequence decided but not communicated clearly, documented properly, or followed through consistently is worse than no consequence at all. A coach who enforces a standard alone carries it personally. A coach who enforces a standard the institution has defined and decided carries it on behalf of something larger — and that difference is what makes enforcement sustainable.
